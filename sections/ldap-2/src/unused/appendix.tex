subsection{TLS characteristics}
\input{tables/ciphersuites}
The RFC9325 gives guidelines for secure use of \gls{TLS} communication. We group and evaluate them by showing the recommended ciphers found in our data set in \cref{tab:tls_ciphersuite}. 

\input{tables/tls_properties}
In general, cipher suites utilizing the \gls{RSA} algorithm for key exchange and authentication are not supported in TLS version 1.3. For version 1.2, they are classified as weak and will soon officially be deprecated, as can be seen in a ``work in progress'' draft by the \gls{IETF}. The reason for this classification is the \todorh{not the algorithm, the session key} falgorithm is non-ephemeral, and thus does not support Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS) \cite{ietf-tls-deprecate-obsolete-kex-03}. Cipher suites using \gls{SHA-1} are classified as weak because \gls{SHA-1} is generally considered vulnerable and was deprecated and disallowed by the \gls{NIST} (its original author) in the year 2011 with SP 800-131A \cite{BarkerRoginsky_TransitioningUseCryptographic_2019}. Cipher suites employing \gls{CBC} mode \cite{fardan2013} are also classified as weak (in \gls{TLS} version 1.2) due to it having vulnerabilities to padding attacks and not providing integrity protection for the encrypted data. \gls{GCM} fixes these specific vulnerabilities and should thus be used in place of \gls{CBC}. Our results are summarized in \cref{tab:tls_properties_table}.

The corresponding field in the \gls{CUIDS} to determine if the certificate is self-signed is computed by comparing issuer- and subject names, and provided in the data set. If both match, Censys automatically categorizes the certificate as self-signed \cite{rfc5280}.
